1 Introductory Concerns

1.4 Prehistoric Developments

Setting the stage When agriculture began, people became gradually more dependant on the food they controlled rather than the food they hunted and gathered. Eventually, completely agricultural villages existed throughout the Near East. In places where rainfall was not optimal for farming, irrigation systems appeared. These prehistoric cultures were spread very widely geographically, and existed for very long periods of time. When communities grew, hierarchies appeared, along with the centralization of powers and functions, the physical focus of which was a building that may already be called a temple.

2 Origins: The Uruk Phenomenon

2.1 The Origins of Cities

What is a city? The Sumerian Uruk, in the very south of Mesopotamia, was the first true city. But what is a city? It has a few characteristics: it is not isolated, being part of an environment that supports it and benefits from it (this also applies to the level of monuments within a city); it supplies special items and services, for which people living around the city rely on it; it is where goods are collected and redistributed; and, being the focal point for everyone in a large area, people go to it to meet each other.

How did settlements progress toward cities? First, in the Ubaid Period, the number of settlements in Mesopotamia gradually increased, and they started to differentiate in size. Then, in the Early Uruk Period, several central settlements in the north of Mesopotamia merged into larger settlements with vast open spaces in between them, while the south, where people had settled throughout the countryside, came to be dominated by one settlement: Uruk. The rapid increase in settled population at this time may be due to increased sedentarization of semi-nomadic people, or from outsiders entering the region. In the Late Uruk Period, so many people lived in Urukâ€™s orbit that some of them must have migrated from western Iran and northern Mesopotamia.

What happened in Uruk that really made it a city? When Uruk had become the center of its surroundings, some people in the city started to specialize in non-agricultural labor. Examples of things made by specialists are the mass-produced beveled-rim bowls and the expertly crafted cylinder seals. The people who made such things did not make their own food, so exchange was necessary. A system emerged where agricultural producers exchanged produce for tools and luxury goods. To organize this, authority was required, the foundation of which was religion, an ideology shared by all the participants. The temple was the central institution that made the system work. To collect and redistribute goods, the temple needed a new class of specialists: administrators. To keep track of things in a complex economy, the administrators needed to record things. To record things, they needed writing.

2.2 The Development of Writing and Administration

Two ways of accounting Accounts provide two sets of data: a record of quantities, and an identification of the people involved in the transaction. The latter was done with seals, first stamp seals, which had been around for a while, then, in the middle of the Uruk period, they were replaced with cylinder seals. There were two ways of recording quantities: the first was with tokens, which denoted a quantity of a particular item. The tokens were kept in bullae, which were hollow spheres of clay with seals rolled over their surface. Problem: you need to break them to see whatâ€™s inside. Measures and numbers In the Late Uruk Period, to make accounting function properly, measures were standardized, and a mixture of the sexagesimal and decimal systems were used for the numerals. Apart from weights and lengths, years and months were also standardized. This system laid the foundation for all later Mesopotamian systems.
